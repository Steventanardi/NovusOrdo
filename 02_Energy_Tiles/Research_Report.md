## 1. Executive Summary
This report explores the feasibility of implementing energy-generating tiles (kinetic floor tiles) within Taiwan's high-traffic transportation hubs, specifically the Taipei MRT and major bus stations. While the technology is currently too expensive to serve as a primary power source for massive infrastructures, it offers significant value as a **Smart City** initiative for localized power and public engagement.

## 2. Technology Overview
Energy tiles convert the mechanical energy of footsteps into electricity using two primary methods:

### A. Electromagnetic Induction (High Efficiency)
*   **Mechanism:** Uses a rack-and-pinion or hydraulic system. When stepped on, the tile moves vertically (approx. 5mm), which rotatates an internal flywheel and generator.
*   **Output:** ~1W to 7W per step (depending on weight/impact).
*   **Maturity:** This is the technology used by market leaders like **Pavegen**. It is more durable and produces higher output.

### B. Piezoelectric Effect (Micro-Power)
*   **Mechanism:** Uses piezoelectric crystals that generate an electric charge when compressed.
*   **Output:** Fraction of a watt.
*   **Use Case:** Ideal for low-power sensors and IoT devices where the mechanical mechanism of electromagnetic tiles would be too bulky.

---

### 2. The Power Profile: How much does 1 Step produce?
Based on 2024-2025 industry benchmarks (Pavegen v3 and modern Induction tiles):
- **Energy per Step**: **2 to 5 Joules (Watt-seconds)**.
- **Peak Power Output**: Up to **7 Watts** per step for electromagnetic induction tiles.
- **Comparison (Piezoelectric)**: Traditional piezoelectric tiles are significantly less efficient, producing only **0.1 to 0.5 Watts** per step.
- **Comparison (TENG)**: Triboelectric Nanogenerators (TENGs) are emerging as high-voltage (30V+) alternatives for self-powered IoT sensors.

### 3. Implementation Focus: Taiwan High-Traffic Areas
Taiwan's unique urban density provides a "High-Yield" environment for kinetic harvesting.
- **MRT Stations (Taipei/Kaohsiung)**: A single commuter walking through a gate (3 steps) produces ~15 Joules. With 2 million daily riders, the potential is **30 Megajoules per day**.
- **Case Study**: **National Cheng Kung University (NCKU)** Hi-GEM Center is currently leading research in TENG-based "Waste Energy" harvesting to power campus IoT networks.

## 3. Feasibility in Taiwan (MRT & Bus Stations)
Taiwan is an ideal environment for this technology due to its extremely high population density and centralized transit hubs.

### Prime Locations
1.  **Taipei Main Station:** The busiest hub in Taiwan with over **300,000 daily commuters**. Corridors near MRT Blue/Red line transfers or the M3 entrance are peak footprint zones.
2.  **MRT Ximen Station:** High foot traffic from younger demographics, making it perfect for "gamified" energy generation displays.
3.  **Taipei City Hall Bus Station:** A major transit point for thousands of commuters daily.

### Practical Application for Taiwan
*   **Powering LEDs:** Lighting up wayfinding paths in underground malls or station exits.
*   **Charging Benches:** Providing "Powered by the People" USB charging stations for commuters' phones.
*   **Real-time Data:** Using the tiles as sensors to track pedestrian flow patterns for station management.
*   **Smart Signs:** Powering electronic paper (E-ink) displays for bus schedules.

## 4. Global Case Studies
*   **West Ham Station, London (2012):** Pilot project for the Olympics. Tiles powered walkway lighting.
*   **Leighton Buzzard Station, UK (2021):** Walkway powering USB charging benches and a data screen.
*   **Shibuya Station, Tokyo:** Experiments using tiles to power interactive LED signs based on crowd density.

## 5. Implementation Roadmap for Taiwan
1.  **Electromagnetic Induction Tiles (Pavegen v3 Standard)**: High-efficiency modular tiles generating **2–5 Joules** per step (Peak **7W**).
2.  **Visual Data Monitoring**: Real-time dashboards showing "Total Carbon Saved" using Taiwan-specific grid emission factors.
3.  **Micro-Grid Integration**: Directing power to MRT signage, emergency exit lights, or **Self-Powered IoT Sensors** (as researched by NCKU).

## 6. Conclusion
Implementing energy tiles in Taiwan is **highly feasible as a pilot project**. While the ROI in terms of "Electricity Saved" vs. "Hardware Cost" is currently low (payback period >15 years), the **PR value, data collection benefits, and Smart City prestige** make it a compelling investment for the Taipei City Government or the Ministry of Transportation.

## 6. Advantages & Disadvantages

### Advantages:
- **Renewable Energy Generation**: Converts mechanical energy from foot traffic into clean electricity.
- **Urban Integration**: Can be installed in existing high-traffic urban areas without requiring massive space like solar or wind farms.
- **Engagement & Data**: Provides real-time data on foot traffic and promotes sustainable behaviors in citizens.

### Disadvantages:
- **High Per-Tile Cost**: 1.  **電磁感應地磚 (Electromagnetic Induction Tiles)**：採用國際 Pavegen v3 標準，每步可產出 **2–5 焦耳 (Joule)** 之能量，峰值功率可達 **7 瓦特 (Watt)**。
2.  **數據監測系統**：即時顯示市民行走產生的「總減碳量」與「發電看板」，結合台灣電力公司排放係數計算。
3.  **微電網整合**：將產生的電力用於捷運站的指示燈、逃生出口標示、或 **NCKU 研發之自供電 IoT 感測器**。
- **Maintenance**: Mechanical parts (springs/magnets) under the tiles require periodic maintenance to prevent friction-based noise or failure.
- **Low Power per Unit**: 1-2 tiles only power light bulbs; thousands are needed for significant industrial power supply.
